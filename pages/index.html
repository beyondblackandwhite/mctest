EXHIBIT TITLE

What is “home”?

What is “home?” “Home” is embedded in this notebook.“Home” is the taste of fresh caught crabs and Old Bay. Home is the sounds and smells of the Celtic Festival, the Jousting Tournament, the Renaissance Festival.

Katie's Story

I bought this miniature embossed leather notebook at the Maryland Renaissance Festival around 2010. It was in my possession for about a year when I left home during my senior year of high school, owing to my parent trying to block me from continuing my education. This was the only item I took from the house. Sometimes I wonder, why this thing?

Stewart/ Authenticity/ Miniature

Surviving miniature books of the Medieval and Renaissance periods were Catholic prayer books. In contrast, modern miniature books sold at Renaissance festivals are not exclusively created or sold for religious purposes. This notebook, for example, contains Islamic influences seen in the folded cover and the natural and geometric patterns on the leather. The inclusion of this stylistic element was not common in Renaissance miniature prayer books, but is more so in Renaissance festival crafts, which developed in the 1960s. This demonstrates Susan Stewart’s argument that “The souvenir displaces the point of authenticity as it itself becomes the point of origin for narrative.”

Even so, Renaissance festival craftsmanship prides itself on authenticity: “The labor was the labor of the hand, of the body, and the product, in its uniqueness, was a stay against Repetition and inauthenticity.” The emphasis on personal craftsmanship and the Renaissance continues to resonate with modern Americans. As historian Paul Grendler argues, the Old West also resonates today. “Americans really love the American West… like the Renaissance, it is viewed as an age of individual achievement.”

Old West

In the popular conception of the American West, leather was used less for books and more for saddles and gun holsters. But there are hidden stories connecting the Old West to the Renaissance embodied in the notebook’s leather cover: stories of colonization, exploitation, and the fusion of cultures. The leather is made from cattle. Cattle need land to graze, and this grazing land in the romanticized “Old West” was taken, often by force, from native tribes. In 1850, Brigham Young said of Native Americans: “Could they be induced to live peacefully and keep herds of cattle, the conditions would very materially be ameliorated, and gradually induce a return to the habits of civilization.” Like the material of the notebook, the Islamic influences in the design speak to this history of exploitation seen in the Reconquista and the Crusades.

But in the popular imagination, it is bold individualism that speak to us from both the Renaissance and the Old West. So, why did I take this thing? The notebook embodies dual histories: of individualism and striking out on your own, but also of dispossession and control.

What is “home”, part 2

So, now, what is home? “Home” is anxiety. “Home” is leaving one Spring afternoon, feeling the sun on your face, the weight lift off your shoulders, and of not looking back. “Home” is a small leather notebook sitting in a dark closet for 6 years. And “home” is keeping an uncomfortable reminder of your past on your bedside table, forcing yourself to look back. And realizing that a notebook is more than a tangible thing. It embodies your past, and your drive toward future. “Home” is looking at a leather notebook and smelling the Chesapeake Bay.
